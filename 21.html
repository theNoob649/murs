Tricky Trials

Minecraft 1.21

  • Released: June 13, 2024
  • Development: 6 snapshots, 4 pre-releases, 1 release candidate

Minecraft has had combat content for years and almost no combat structures. Strongholds are a corridor to the End. Woodland mansions are a loot run with bad pathing. 1.21 built the first structure designed from the ground up as a fight you enter on purpose and leave when you decide to.

Trial Chambers

Trial chambers generate in the deepslate layers as modular tuff-and-copper complexes assembled from a pool of room types, so no two are laid out the same. They are lit, they are visible on the map, and they are non-hostile until you engage them.

  • Trial spawners are the core mechanic. Unlike a normal spawner, one activates when a player approaches, releases a fixed number of mobs scaled to how many players are present, then permanently exhausts itself and ejects loot. You clear a room once. It stays cleared.
  • Vaults hold the real reward. Each vault needs a trial key dropped by spawners in that chamber, and every player gets their own roll from the same vault — nobody loses out to whoever sprinted ahead.
  • Room variety: chambers include melee arenas, ranged platforms, spike traps, dispenser corridors, and treasure rooms, plus decorated pots and barrels salted through as minor loot.

Ominous Events

1.21 reworked the Bad Omen effect into an item. Drinking an ominous bottle, or getting Bad Omen from a raid captain, gives you a choice about difficulty.

  • Ominous trial spawners spawn more and tougher mobs, keep spawning on a timer instead of a fixed count, and can apply potion effects to what they release. Their ominous vaults hold the best loot in the update.
  • Ominous vaults require ominous trial keys, obtainable only from ominous spawners — the difficulty and the reward are locked to each other.
  • Four new potion effects arrived with it: Wind Charged, Weaving, Oozing, and Infested — all of which turn the player's own death or damage into an additional hazard.

The Breeze, the Mace, and Wind

The Breeze is the update's signature mob and its most unusual one. It does not melee. It bounces around the arena firing wind charges that deal little damage but knock you into traps, off ledges, and out of position. It is a positioning problem, not a damage problem — and its wind charges also flip levers, press buttons, and open doors, which is why redstone builders adopted it immediately.

The mace is crafted from a heavy core — the rarest drop from ominous vaults — plus a breeze rod. Its damage scales with how far you fell before landing the hit, and a successful mace hit cancels your own fall damage entirely. Combined with wind charges launching you upward, it created a genuinely new combat loop: get airborne, come down on something.

  • The Bogged: a mossy skeleton variant firing poison arrows, spawning in swamps, mangrove swamps, and trial chambers.
  • The Crafter: a redstone-activated crafting block. Fully automatic crafting, requested for over a decade.
  • New copper and tuff block families — including copper bulbs, which toggle light level on a redstone pulse and oxidize into dimmer stages.

It was also the end of an era. Tricky Trials was the last of the big annual updates. Everything that followed arrived as a game drop — smaller, named, themed releases shipping three to four times a year.

A common mix-up: the Happy Ghast (1.21.6), the Copper Golem and copper gear (1.21.9), and the Pale Garden (1.21.4) are not part of Tricky Trials. They share the 1.21.x version prefix, but each is its own separate drop with its own announcement and development cycle.

See Chase the Skies, The Copper Age, and The Garden Awakens.

Ten years of players asking for repeatable, scalable combat content. 1.21 is the answer, and it arrived just in time to be the last one of its kind.
