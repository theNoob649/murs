The Frostburn Update

Minecraft 1.10

  • Released: June 8, 2016
  • Development: 3 snapshots, 2 pre-releases

1.10 is the smallest major update Minecraft has ever shipped. Three snapshots, twenty-one days from the first one to release — fewer development builds than any other numbered version. The name is a portmanteau of frost and burn, and the update is exactly that: a small pile of cold things and a small pile of hot things.

The Cold Half

Snowy biomes had almost nothing distinguishing them beyond the colour of the ground. 1.10 gave them their own mobs.

  • Polar bears spawn in ice and snowy biomes. Adults alone are neutral, but any adult with a cub nearby is hostile on approach — one of the few mobs whose aggression depends on what else is standing next to it.
  • Strays are skeletons that spawn in frozen biomes, wear tattered cloaks, and fire arrows of Slowness. They are the only renewable source of Slowness arrows.
  • Husks are the desert counterpart: zombies that survive daylight without burning and inflict Hunger on hit. Because they do not burn at sunrise, deserts stay dangerous around the clock.

Strays and husks are biome-locked replacements for their base mobs rather than additions alongside them, and that is the quietly meaningful part — it was the first time the game made ordinary hostile mobs feel regional rather than uniform.

The Hot Half

  • Magma blocks deal damage every second to anything standing on them, similar to cactus but without destroying dropped items. They generate in the Nether and, notably, at the bottom of underwater ravines.
  • Nether wart blocks, made from nine nether wart, giving a deep red spongy texture that builders use far more for its colour than its origin.
  • Red nether bricks, crafted from nether brick and nether wart, filling an obvious gap in the Nether palette.
  • Bone blocks, compact bone meal storage, and the block that fossils are made of — large skeletal structures that generate underground in deserts and swamps, the first purely decorative buried feature in the game.

Quality of Life and Tools

  • Structure blocks became usable in creative, letting players save, load, and copy builds directly in-game. They are how Mojang itself authors structures, and they made large-scale building work portable between worlds.
  • Auto-jump, on by default at launch, which lets the player step up single blocks without pressing space. It was immediately divisive and immediately turned off by a large share of players.
  • The /teleport command gained relative rotation arguments.
  • Mob AI got a pass — skeletons hold their bows differently, zombie pigmen behave more consistently in groups, and mob spawning was retuned.

Context: 1.10 landed just three months after 1.9, in the middle of Mojang's shift toward smaller, faster releases. It reads less like a themed update and more like the leftovers of one — useful, but nobody's favourite.

Three weeks of development, and a handful of blocks that quietly stayed useful.
