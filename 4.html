The Pretty Scary Update

Minecraft 1.4

  • Released: October 25, 2012
  • Development: 15 snapshots, 3 pre-releases

Timed for Halloween and named accordingly, 1.4 is the update that gave Minecraft its second boss, its first endgame status-effect block, and the command block — a tool Mojang added for map makers that ended up defining an entire genre of server.

The Wither

The Ender Dragon had been the only boss for three years, and beating it ended the game. The Wither is the opposite: you build it deliberately, in the Overworld, whenever you want.

The recipe is four blocks of soul sand in a T shape topped with three wither skeleton skulls — and wither skeletons only spawn in nether fortresses and rarely drop their heads, so assembling one is a project in itself. Once summoned it charges an explosive detonation, then flies, shoots skulls that inflict the Wither effect, and destroys terrain as it goes.

Its drop, the nether star, exists for exactly one purpose.

Beacons

  • A nether star plus glass and obsidian makes a beacon, placed atop a pyramid of iron, gold, emerald, or diamond blocks.
  • The pyramid's size determines range and which effects you can pick: Speed, Haste, Resistance, Jump Boost, or Strength, with a secondary slot at full size.
  • A full four-tier pyramid costs 164 blocks of mineral. It is the game's most expensive build by a wide margin and its clearest statement about what "endgame" means — permanent, area-wide buffs for a base you never plan to leave.

Command Blocks and Map Making

Command blocks execute a server command when powered by redstone. Mojang added them to support the custom adventure maps players were already building by hand, and the effect was much larger than intended: combined with 1.3's adventure mode, they made it possible to script entire minigames inside vanilla Minecraft with no mods and no plugins. Every large minigame server lineage traces back to this.

Everything Else

  • Anvils: repair, rename, and combine enchanted items using experience — the first way to preserve gear you cared about instead of re-rolling it.
  • New mobs: witches, which throw splash potions and drink them defensively; bats for cave ambience; zombie villagers, which can be cured with a splash potion of weakness and a golden apple; and wither skeletons.
  • Item frames and flower pots for decoration, and mob heads as trophies.
  • Carrots and potatoes as new crops, plus pumpkin pie and the carrot on a stick for steering saddled pigs.
  • Block placement sounds for wood, stone, plant, sand, and gravel — a small addition that noticeably changed how the game feels.
  • Armored mobs: hostile mobs can now spawn wearing armor and holding tools, and use them.
  • Village reputation: villagers track how you have treated them, and iron golems respond accordingly.
  • Maps became craftable, copyable, and extendable, and leather armor became dyeable across millions of color combinations.

The sleeper feature: command blocks were a convenience for adventure-map authors. Within a couple of years they were the backbone of the entire vanilla minigame ecosystem, and Mojang has been expanding the command system ever since.

A Halloween update that accidentally handed the community a scripting language.
