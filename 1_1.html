Minecraft 1.1

Spawn Eggs, Superflat, and a New Lead Developer

  • Released: January 12, 2012
  • Development: 5 snapshots (11w47a through 12w01a)

1.1 had no official name and was never marketed as a headline update, which undersells how significant it was procedurally. It was the first version developed under Jens Bergensten as lead creative developer after Notch stepped back, and the first version built in public through weekly snapshots — the development model Minecraft has used ever since.

The Snapshot Era Begins

Before 1.1, players saw a build when Mojang decided it was ready. Starting with 11w47a, Mojang began pushing unfinished development builds most Thursdays, named by year, week, and letter. Anyone could opt in, break things, and file bugs.

The effect was less about any single feature than about pace and feedback. Community reaction now arrived days after a change instead of months, and the wiki, the mod ecosystem, and the bug tracker all reorganized themselves around the weekly rhythm.

Creative Mode Gets Real Tools

  • Spawn eggs: 20 varieties, creative-only at the time, letting builders and map makers place mobs directly instead of engineering spawn conditions for them.
  • Superflat worlds: a new world type generating a featureless layered plane. It became the default canvas for redstone testing, minigame maps, and command experiments.
  • Void fog removed from creative mode, the Nether, and the End, making deep building considerably less miserable.

World Generation and Bow Enchantments

Beaches returned as a proper biome rather than an accident of terrain, and new variants appeared including desert hills and frozen ocean. The underlying generator was still the Beta 1.8 one, but it produced noticeably more varied coastlines.

Bows finally got their own enchantments — four of them, and they have barely changed since:

  • Power — flat damage increase, the standard ranged upgrade.
  • Punch — adds knockback on hit.
  • Flame — sets targets on fire.
  • Infinity — shoot indefinitely from a single arrow.

Quality of Life

A cluster of small balance changes landed that players still feel today. Farmland stopped being destroyed by walking over it — you now have to jump on it to trample it, which saved countless accidentally ruined farms. Armor durability was increased substantially across every material. Golden apples were reworked to restore health and grant Regeneration rather than acting as ordinary food. Brewing time dropped from 30 seconds to 20.

1.1 also shipped 56 new translations at once, taking the game from a handful of languages to broad international coverage in a single release.

The lasting change: nothing in 1.1's feature list rivals the End or enchanting. But the snapshot system it introduced shaped every update that followed, and superflat quietly became the foundation the entire command-block and minigame scene was built on.

A small update by content, and a structural one by consequence.
