Minecraft Pre-Classic

rd-132211 — rd-161348

  • Period: May 10, 2009 — May 16, 2009
  • Key versions: rd-132211, rd-132328, rd-20090515, rd-160052, rd-161348

Minecraft's entire Pre-Classic era lasted less than a week. Markus "Notch" Persson began work on May 10, 2009, and by May 16 the project had already crossed into what we now call Classic. Almost nobody played these builds at the time — access was limited to Notch himself and a tester known as "ez." Everything that followed grew out of these few days.

Cave Game

The project didn't start out named Minecraft. Notch called it Cave Game, then briefly Minecraft: Order of the Stone, before settling on plain "Minecraft."

It also didn't start from nothing. The rd- prefix on every Pre-Classic version stands for RubyDung, an earlier project of Notch's whose rendering code Minecraft reused. The digits after the prefix aren't a version count at all — they're a timestamp in Stockholm time, encoded as day, hour, and minute. rd-132211 means the 13th of the month, 22:11.

What Actually Existed

Very little, but the important part was there from the first build.

  • Placing and breaking blocks. Grass and stone, on a grid, in first person. This is the whole game in one sentence, and it worked on day one.
  • Procedurally generated terrain — not the endless worlds of later years, but generated rather than hand-authored.
  • Cobblestone, added partway through the week, giving mined stone a distinct form.
  • Mobs, spawned manually with the G key. They were placeholder humanoids that wandered without purpose or hostility.
  • Respawning via the R key, and basic world saving, so a build could survive the game being closed.
  • Particle effects when blocks broke — a small touch that has never left the game.

Why It Mattered

Pre-Classic tested the core loop before anything was stacked on top of it. There was no health, no crafting, no inventory, no survival, no goal. There was a world made of cubes you could take apart and put back together, and that turned out to be enough to hold a player's attention.

Every system Minecraft added afterward — furnaces, redstone, the Nether, villages, enchanting — rests on the assumption that placing and breaking blocks is inherently satisfying. That assumption was confirmed in roughly seventy-two hours in May 2009.

On the timestamps: because rd- versions are clock stamps rather than sequence numbers, you can read Notch's working hours straight off the version list. rd-132211 and rd-132328 were compiled about an hour apart, late at night.

A week of work, a borrowed engine, and a name he didn't keep. Pre-Classic is the shortest era in Minecraft's history and the only one without which none of the others exist.
