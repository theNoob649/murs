Minecraft Classic

mc-161607 — c0.30

  • Period: May 16, 2009 — November 10, 2009
  • Key versions: mc-161607, c0.0.11a, c0.24_SURVIVAL_TEST, c0.30

Classic is where Minecraft stopped being a private side project and became something strangers played. It ran as a Java applet in a browser, it was free, and it spread through forums and word of mouth long before any storefront had heard of it. Six months of near-daily updates turned a block-placing toy into a game with mobs, damage, and a reason to fear the dark.

Free, In A Browser, Multiplayer

The first public build went up on May 17, 2009. No download, no install, no account — just a web page. That distribution model did an enormous amount of the early marketing on its own: sharing Minecraft meant sharing a link.

Multiplayer arrived on May 31, 2009, barely two weeks in, and it reshaped the community immediately. Players began running their own servers and building collaboratively, and the culture of the public build server — still recognizable today — formed here.

Survival Test

For its first several months Classic had no danger at all. That changed on September 1, 2009, with c0.24_SURVIVAL_TEST, a separate mode introducing the mechanics the rest of the game would be built around.

  • An empty inventory. You start with nothing and mine your materials rather than picking them from a palette.
  • Health and death, with damage from falling, drowning, and combat.
  • Hostile mobs: zombies, skeletons, creepers, and spiders — the original four, all still in the game.
  • Mob drops and scoring, the first hint of progression.

Survival Test ran alongside the creative version rather than replacing it, so for the last stretch of Classic there were effectively two Minecrafts sharing an engine.

The Block Palette

Classic assembled most of the vocabulary later versions would rely on. Water and lava got flow simulation. Sand and gravel got gravity. Ores appeared in stone, glass could be seen through, and wool arrived in a full spread of colors that made large-scale pixel art possible for the first time. Late Classic added obsidian and sheep, closing out the era in November 2009.

Worlds were finite — a fixed slab of terrain ringed by ocean. Infinite generation was still a year away, and the constraint arguably helped: a Classic map was small enough that a server's regulars could actually fill it.

Still playable: Classic 0.30 remains available in-browser through Mojang's own archived release, which is unusual for a build this old. It is the earliest version of Minecraft most people can genuinely try.

Classic gave Minecraft its first audience, its multiplayer culture, and — through Survival Test — the first rough sketch of the survival loop that everything after it refined.
